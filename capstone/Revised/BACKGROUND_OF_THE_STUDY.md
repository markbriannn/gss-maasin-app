# BACKGROUND OF THE STUDY

In today's digital age, the demand for sideline services such as carpentry, electrical work, plumbing, and cleaning continues to grow. These skilled workers play a crucial role in the daily lives of communities, providing essential support to households and small businesses. However, the ways in which these services are promoted and accessed remain largely informal and outdated. Information, which has become a vital resource, is often unmanaged or fragmented, limiting opportunities for both providers and clients.

Communities have long recognized the importance of managing key resources such as skilled labor, materials, and capital. Information has now moved to its rightful place as a key resource alongside these traditional assets. Service providers and clients are beginning to understand that information is not just a by-product of conducting transactions; rather, it fuels economic activity and can be the critical factor in determining the success or failure of service delivery. To maximize the usefulness of information in service delivery, communities must manage it correctly, just as they manage other resources. Managers and decision-makers need to understand that costs are associated with the production, distribution, security, storage, and retrieval of information. The strategic use of information for connecting service providers with clients efficiently and positioning service businesses competitively should not be taken for granted.

This research focuses on Maasin City, Southern Leyte, where sideline service providers are deeply embedded in barangay life. Maasin City, the capital of Southern Leyte province, serves as the commercial, educational, and residential center of the region. Established as a city in 2000, Maasin has experienced steady population growth and economic development over the past two decades. The city is home to approximately 87,000 residents as of the latest census, with a diverse economy that includes agriculture, commerce, education, and services.

Within Maasin City's economy, sideline service providers constitute a significant informal sector. These workers—carpenters, electricians, plumbers, cleaners, appliance repair technicians, and other skilled tradespeople—provide essential services to households, small businesses, and institutions throughout the city. Traditionally, service exchanges have relied on personal connections and word-of-mouth, which work well within close-knit communities. During the city's early years of development, when the population was smaller and communities were more tightly knit, information management about these services was not a significant challenge. Residents knew their neighbors, word-of-mouth recommendations were reliable, and service providers could easily be found through personal connections within barangays. Almost all residents within a barangay knew each other, and clients could easily recognize and contact service providers by their names or through mutual acquaintances. Thus, if a resident needed a carpenter or electrician, they could quickly find one through their immediate network, and the service provider's reputation was well-known within the community.

At present, this situation no longer holds true. As Maasin City's population has grown to approximately 87,000 residents, with continued expansion and increasing diversity, the traditional informal methods are no longer sufficient. The city now has a significantly larger and more mobile population, with residents coming from various barangays and even from other municipalities. Sometimes, during peak seasons or special events, the demand for services increases dramatically, yet the community lacks an efficient way to mobilize its pool of skilled workers to meet this demand.

Sadly, because of poor information management, the leadership and residents are unable to efficiently connect with available service providers. A skilled electrician living in one barangay may be unknown to residents in another barangay who desperately need electrical services. This made it nearly impossible for clients to find appropriate providers beyond their immediate social circles. Additionally, a regular client who successfully hired a provider once may struggle to contact that same provider again if they did not save contact information, as there is no centralized directory or booking system. Only those service providers with strong personal networks or who are already well-known in multiple barangays can be easily found and consequently they are the only workers who receive consistent income opportunities. The majority of skilled workers remain underutilized and unknown outside their traditional circles.

The current situation reveals a significant problem: there is no centralized or organized way to access reliable information about sideline service providers, their skills, availability, or reputation. This lack of accessible, verified data leads to difficulties in decision-making for clients, missed income opportunities for workers, and overall inefficiency in service delivery. The absence of a centralized information system creates multiple challenges. Service providers miss income opportunities because potential clients cannot find them. Clients experience frustration searching for appropriate providers and often settle for whoever they can find through limited personal connections, regardless of whether that provider is the best match for their needs. The lack of formal verification mechanisms raises serious concerns about safety and trust, as clients have no way to confirm provider credentials or background before inviting them into their homes or businesses.

Additionally, the informal nature of transactions—typically conducted in cash without documentation—creates problems for both parties. Service providers have difficulty tracking their earnings and building a verifiable work history. Clients lack receipts for their transactions and have no recourse for dispute resolution. The entire process, from finding a provider to completing payment, remains fragmented, inefficient, and vulnerable to miscommunication and misunderstanding. The community's economic potential remains unrealized, with skilled workers underemployed and residents unable to access needed services efficiently. Decision-making about hiring services is consequently done under uncertainty, with clients taking unnecessary risks and providers missing opportunities to grow their businesses.

To better understand these challenges, surveys and interviews were conducted with local sideline workers and service seekers in Maasin City. The results highlighted a clear need for improved methods of connecting providers and clients, enhancing trust, and increasing access to services. Service providers expressed frustration with their limited ability to reach new clients and grow their businesses beyond their immediate networks. They indicated strong interest in a platform that could help them showcase their skills, manage bookings efficiently, and receive payments securely. Clients, on the other hand, emphasized the need for a reliable way to find qualified providers, verify their credentials before hiring, and book services with confidence. Both groups identified the lack of a centralized, trustworthy platform as a significant barrier to efficient service delivery in the city.

It is for these reasons that the researchers, being residents of Maasin City and having witnessed firsthand the challenges faced by both service providers and clients, have gained a deep desire to find a better way of improving the present condition by developing a comprehensive platform called the "General Service System" (GSS). This system will help solve the unsatisfactory state of service delivery and information management in Maasin City. The General Service System addresses the identified problems through a technology-enabled approach that brings organization, transparency, and efficiency to the informal service economy.

The system provides service providers with a platform to showcase their skills, build verifiable reputations through client reviews, manage their bookings efficiently, and receive payments securely through PayMongo integration supporting GCash and credit/debit card transactions. For clients, the system offers a reliable way to find qualified providers, verify their credentials through administrative review of government-issued IDs, barangay clearance, and police clearance, compare options based on ratings and reviews, and book services with confidence. Real-time GPS tracking during service delivery provides transparency and security for both parties, while the feedback and rating system promotes accountability and helps future clients make informed decisions.

The development of this system represents more than a technological solution; it is an intervention designed to transform how the Maasin City community connects service providers with clients. By formalizing the informal service economy through a user-friendly platform accessible via both web and mobile applications, the research seeks to demonstrate how technology can be adapted to local contexts to solve real-world problems, create economic opportunities, and improve the quality of life for community members.
