# THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

This capstone project is anchored in several well-established theories that support the planning, development, and evaluation of the General Service System. These theories collectively justify the technical, behavioral, and social aspects of the platform, providing a comprehensive foundation for understanding how the system addresses the identified problems in Maasin City's sideline service economy.

## Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)

Developed by Davis (1989), the Technology Acceptance Model explains how users come to accept and use a new technology. The model focuses on two main factors that significantly influence user attitudes and behavior: Perceived Usefulness, which refers to the degree to which a person believes that using a particular system would enhance their performance, and Perceived Ease of Use, which refers to the degree to which a person believes that using the system would be free of effort.

In this project, TAM provides the theoretical foundation for designing a user-friendly interface and efficient functionality that increases the likelihood of adoption among both service providers and clients. The system's design prioritizes intuitive navigation, clear visual feedback, and streamlined processes for booking, payment, and communication. By ensuring that users perceive the platform as both useful for solving their problems and easy to operate, the research applies TAM principles to maximize user acceptance and sustained engagement with the General Service System.

## Diffusion of Innovation Theory

Rogers' (2003) Diffusion of Innovation Theory outlines how a new idea, product, or technology spreads through a population over time. The theory identifies five attributes that influence the rate of adoption: Relative Advantage, the degree to which an innovation is perceived as better than the idea it supersedes; Compatibility, the degree to which an innovation is perceived as consistent with existing values, experiences, and needs; Complexity, the degree to which an innovation is perceived as difficult to understand and use; Trialability, the degree to which an innovation may be experimented with on a limited basis; and Observability, the degree to which the results of an innovation are visible to others.

These principles guide the development of features that make the General Service System easy to use, socially beneficial, and highly visible, encouraging adoption across various communities in Maasin City. The platform demonstrates clear relative advantage over traditional word-of-mouth methods by providing centralized access to verified providers, secure payment processing, and transparent feedback systems. Its compatibility with existing social practices is maintained through familiar booking processes and communication patterns. The system's low complexity is achieved through intuitive design and onboarding guidance. Trialability is supported by allowing users to browse providers and view ratings before committing to bookings. Finally, observability is enhanced through visible success indicators such as completed transactions, positive reviews, and growing provider networks, which encourage others to adopt the platform.

## Integrative Model of Organizational Trust

Trust is critical in sideline service platforms where users rely on provider profiles, ratings, and reviews to make hiring decisions. According to Mayer, Davis, and Schoorman's (1995) Integrative Model of Organizational Trust, trust is built on three fundamental dimensions: Ability, the perception that the trustee has the skills and competencies to perform as expected; Integrity, the perception that the trustee adheres to a set of principles that the trustor finds acceptable; and Benevolence, the perception that the trustee wants to do good to the trustor, aside from an egocentric profit motive.

The General Service System design reflects these trust-building principles through multiple mechanisms. Ability is demonstrated through provider profiles that showcase skills, experience, and verified credentials. Integrity is established through multi-level identity verification including government-issued IDs, profile photographs, barangay clearance, and police clearance, ensuring that providers are who they claim to be and have clean records. Benevolence is fostered through transparent feedback systems where both clients and providers can rate each other, secure communication channels that protect user privacy, and fair dispute resolution processes. These features collectively promote safety and credibility, addressing the trust deficit that has long plagued informal service arrangements in the community.

## ISO/IEC 9126 Software Quality Model

To assess and ensure software quality, this study utilizes the ISO/IEC 9126 framework established by the International Organization for Standardization (2001). This internationally recognized standard evaluates software based on six quality characteristics: Functionality, the capability of the software to provide functions that meet stated and implied needs; Reliability, the capability to maintain a specified level of performance under stated conditions; Usability, the effort needed for use and the individual assessment of such use by a stated or implied set of users; Efficiency, the relationship between the level of performance and the amount of resources used; Maintainability, the effort needed to make specified modifications; and Portability, the ability to be transferred from one environment to another.

This model is directly relevant for determining whether the developed General Service System meets international standards and provides a high-quality experience to users. The evaluation framework guides both the development process and the assessment methodology, ensuring that the platform is not only functional but also reliable, user-friendly, efficient, maintainable, and portable across different devices and platforms. By applying ISO/IEC 9126 criteria, the research establishes objective measures for system quality that can be validated through systematic testing and user evaluation, providing credible evidence of the platform's effectiveness in addressing the identified problems in Maasin City's service delivery ecosystem.

## Synthesis of Theoretical Foundations

Together, these four theoretical frameworks provide a comprehensive foundation for the General Service System. TAM ensures that the system is designed for user acceptance, Diffusion of Innovation Theory guides strategies for widespread adoption, the Integrative Model of Organizational Trust addresses the critical issue of safety and credibility in peer-to-peer service transactions, and ISO/IEC 9126 provides objective criteria for evaluating system quality. This multi-theoretical approach acknowledges that successful technology implementation requires attention to user psychology, social dynamics, trust mechanisms, and technical excellence, all of which are essential for transforming Maasin City's informal service economy into an organized, efficient, and trustworthy marketplace.
