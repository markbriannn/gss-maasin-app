# REVIEW OF RELATED LITERATURE

The development of the General Service System (GSS) is anchored in established research on digital labor platforms, informal employment systems, technology adoption, trust formation, and software quality. This review synthesizes relevant theories and empirical studies to provide a solid academic foundation for the project, identify existing gaps in the literature, and align previous findings with the objectives of the GSS. Emphasis is placed on online job posting and job hunting systems, informal labor markets in rural contexts, and the role of technology in improving service accessibility and efficiency.

## Digital Platforms and Online Job Systems

Advancements in information and communication technology (ICT) have fundamentally transformed how employment opportunities are created, accessed, and managed. Online job publication systems enable employers and job seekers to interact through centralized platforms where job vacancies can be posted, searched, and applied for electronically. According to Sagapsapan and Campiseño (2018), such systems reduce information asymmetry, lower recruitment costs, and improve transparency in the labor market. Their study demonstrated that web-based job publication systems significantly improve recruitment efficiency by automating applicant screening and organizing employment information in accessible formats.

Similar findings are reflected in broader labor studies conducted by international organizations. Digital labor platforms streamline recruitment and service matching by minimizing manual coordination and expanding market reach beyond traditional geographic and social boundaries (International Labour Organization [ILO], 2018). These platforms allow workers to present their skills, availability, and credentials in a structured manner while enabling clients to identify suitable service providers efficiently through search and filtering mechanisms. However, most existing systems are designed primarily for formal employment relationships and urban environments, limiting their applicability in rural and informal labor settings where different social dynamics and infrastructure constraints exist.

## Informal Labor Markets and Rural Employment Contexts

Informal labor markets are characterized by non-contractual, short-term, and flexible work arrangements that operate outside formal regulatory frameworks. In rural communities, employment opportunities are often accessed through personal referrals and word-of-mouth networks that rely heavily on existing social relationships. While these traditional methods foster trust through personal familiarity and shared community membership, they significantly limit service visibility and restrict opportunities for workers outside immediate social circles. The ILO (2018) emphasized that informal workers face significant barriers to stable income due to the lack of structured job-matching mechanisms and the limited reach of traditional networking methods.

Research on peer-to-peer marketplaces indicates that digital platforms can reduce these barriers by lowering coordination costs and enabling flexible participation in service provision without requiring formal employment contracts (Einav et al., 2016). The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD, 2019) further reported that digital labor platforms allow informal workers to diversify income sources and improve job accessibility by connecting them with clients beyond their immediate geographic and social networks. Despite these advantages, most platforms prioritize urban users with reliable internet access, digital literacy, and established digital payment systems, leaving rural areas such as Maasin City underserved. The GSS addresses this limitation by offering a localized, web-based solution tailored to low-resource environments with consideration for intermittent connectivity and varying levels of digital literacy.

## Technology Adoption in Informal Labor Markets

User acceptance is a critical determinant of the success of digital platforms, particularly in contexts where potential users have limited prior experience with technology-mediated transactions. Davis' (1989) Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) posits that perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use strongly influence technology adoption decisions. In informal labor contexts, workers are more likely to adopt systems that simplify existing processes and clearly improve income opportunities without imposing excessive learning burdens (Venkatesh et al., 2003). Studies on peer-to-peer platforms further support this view, indicating that intuitive interfaces and transparent job-matching features increase participation rates and sustained engagement (Fradkin et al., 2021).

The GSS aligns with TAM principles by simplifying registration procedures, minimizing required user inputs during the booking process, and providing clear job-matching filters based on skills, service categories, and availability. These features are specifically designed to accommodate users with varying levels of digital literacy, increasing the likelihood of sustained system use across different demographic groups within Maasin City. By reducing complexity and emphasizing immediate practical benefits, the platform addresses key adoption barriers identified in the literature.

## Diffusion of Innovation and Platform Adoption

Rogers' (2003) Diffusion of Innovation (DOI) theory explains how new technologies spread through populations based on five key attributes: relative advantage, compatibility, complexity, trialability, and observability. The GSS incorporates these attributes strategically by offering faster and more reliable service matching than traditional word-of-mouth referrals, ensuring compatibility with mobile browsers and various devices, allowing free registration for service providers to reduce entry barriers, and displaying visible ratings and verification badges that make system benefits observable to potential users.

While DOI has been widely applied in urban technology adoption studies, particularly in developed country contexts, its application in rural, low-technology environments remains limited. By implementing DOI principles in a rural informal labor setting, the GSS contributes empirical insights into how digital platforms can diffuse within community-based economies where social networks and traditional practices remain strong. This research extends DOI theory by examining adoption patterns in contexts where digital and traditional systems must coexist and complement each other.

## Peer-to-Peer Marketplaces and Platform Efficiency

Peer-to-peer service platforms have demonstrated their ability to improve labor allocation efficiency and reduce transaction costs by eliminating intermediaries and enabling direct connections between service providers and clients. Einav et al. (2016) found that these platforms enable individuals to act flexibly as both service providers and consumers, lowering entry barriers for informal workers who lack formal credentials or established business infrastructure. Employment studies further suggest that peer-to-peer platforms contribute to modest employment growth by supporting short-term and on-demand work arrangements that complement traditional employment (OECD, 2019; Fradkin et al., 2021).

Key platform features such as skill-based filtering, transparent service listings with detailed provider profiles, and feedback mechanisms enhance accountability and service quality by making provider performance visible and consequential (Revista Future, 2023). These findings informed the design of the GSS, particularly in its localized service matching algorithms and user rating components that adapt global platform best practices to the specific context of Maasin City's service economy.

## Trust, Credibility, and Security in Digital Platforms

Trust is a fundamental requirement for the success of freelance and service-oriented platforms, particularly in contexts where users must invite service providers into their homes or businesses. Mayer et al. (1995) identified ability, integrity, and benevolence as the core components of trust in organizational relationships. In peer-to-peer platforms, identity verification and reputation systems significantly improve user confidence and retention by providing credible signals about provider quality and reliability (Einav et al., 2016).

Unlike global platforms that rely primarily on algorithmic trust scores and user-generated reviews, the GSS adopts a community-centered trust model that combines digital verification with locally meaningful credibility signals. Service providers undergo multi-level verification using government-issued IDs, profile photographs for identity confirmation, Barangay Clearance to verify community standing, and Police Clearance to confirm the absence of criminal records. Transparent feedback and admin-monitored rating systems further reinforce credibility while aligning with rural cultural norms where personal reputation is highly valued and community accountability mechanisms remain strong.

Security and privacy are also critical considerations in platform design. Research emphasizes the importance of authentication, encryption, and controlled data exposure in protecting users from fraud and misuse (Expert Systems with Applications, 2014; Applied Energy, 2023). These measures are particularly important for informal workers who often lack formal legal protections and may be vulnerable to exploitation or identity theft. The GSS implements security measures appropriate to its context, balancing protection with accessibility for users who may have limited experience with digital security practices.

## Software Quality and Usability Standards

Software quality plays a vital role in system reliability and user satisfaction, particularly in environments where technical support may be limited and users have low tolerance for system failures. The ISO/IEC 9126 standard provides a comprehensive framework for evaluating software quality based on six characteristics: functionality, usability, reliability, efficiency, maintainability, and portability (ISO/IEC, 2001). Sagapsapan and Campiseño (2018) demonstrated that online job systems evaluated using this standard achieved high levels of user acceptability and sustained usage rates.

The GSS applies ISO/IEC 9126 principles to ensure reliable operation under intermittent internet connectivity, mobile-responsive design that adapts to various screen sizes and devices, and ease of use for users with limited technical experience. By adapting international quality standards to a rural context with specific infrastructure constraints, the GSS extends software quality research to resource-constrained environments and demonstrates how global standards can be meaningfully applied in developing country contexts.

## Research Gaps and Contribution

Despite extensive literature on digital labor platforms, significant gaps remain in understanding how these systems can be effectively adapted to rural informal labor markets. Most studies focus on urban and formal employment settings in developed countries, with limited attention to rural informal labor markets in developing regions where infrastructure, literacy levels, and social practices differ substantially. There is also a lack of research on hybrid platforms that combine digital coordination with offline practices such as face-to-face interactions, cash payments, and community-based trust mechanisms that remain important in many rural contexts.

The GSS addresses these gaps by providing a localized, hybrid, and trust-aware platform specifically tailored to Maasin City's unique context. By integrating technology adoption theories, peer-to-peer marketplace insights, trust frameworks, and software quality standards, the system contributes to the growing body of research on inclusive digital labor solutions. This research demonstrates how global platform concepts can be adapted to local contexts while respecting existing social practices and working within infrastructure constraints. The findings will provide valuable insights for researchers and practitioners seeking to develop similar platforms in other rural and informal labor market contexts.
